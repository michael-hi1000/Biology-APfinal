Cultivated Plants

Exploring how humans propagate and improve plants through asexual reproduction

What are Cultivated Plants?

Many common cultivated plants rely on asexual reproduction for their propagation and improvement. Key agricultural and horticultural species such as potatoes, strawberries, pineapples, and bananas are propagated through suckers and runners.

Bulbous plants like daffodils, lilies, and crocuses also regenerate from their storage organs. Furthermore, high-value fruit crops including seedless grapes and navel oranges are exclusively cultivated through human-guided techniques like grafting to maintain their desirable, consistent qualities.

Propagation Methods

Suckers & Runners

  • Pineapples and bananas reproduce through shoots (suckers)
  • Strawberries spread via runners (stolons)
  • These are stems that grow horizontally and develop roots
  • Eventually separate from parent plant to form new individuals

Storage Organs

  • Potatoes grow from tubers (underground stems)
  • Daffodils, lilies, and crocuses grow from bulbs
  • These organs store nutrients for the new plant
  • Buds on these organs develop into new plants

Artificial Techniques

  • Grafting joins parts from two different plants
  • Used for seedless grapes and navel oranges
  • Maintains consistent fruit quality
  • Preserves desirable traits indefinitely

Examples of Cultivated Plants

Potatoes

Grown from tubers, which are underground stem structures that store starch and contain buds for new plants.

Pineapples

Reproduced through suckers that develop as outgrowths from the stem or from the spiky crown.

Benefits of Asexual Propagation

  • Genetic consistency: All plants have identical traits to the parent
  • Preservation of desirable traits: Excellent flavor, size, or appearance maintained
  • Faster maturity: Plants reach productive stage more quickly than from seed
  • Disease-free start: Can begin with certified disease-free planting material
  • Seedless varieties: Allows cultivation of plants that don't produce viable seeds
